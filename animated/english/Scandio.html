Scandium

The name of scandium (Sc) comes from Scandinavia, since it was discovered by Swedish chemist Nilson in 1879 while searching for rare earth metals. Rare earths were found mostly in mineral oxides associated with gadolinite, extracted from a mine in the village of Ytterby in Sweden. Scandium and Yttrium have chemical properties similar to those of rare earths. Scandium is a silvery white, light metal that turns yellow or pinkish when exposed to air. Within its group (Sc, Y, La) is the element with the lowest density and is sufficiently tender to be cut with a knife. It reacts with water to release hydrogen and its metal powder is flammable in the air. It is used mainly in alloys with aluminum as it increases its strength and in high brightness lamps.
